Lipofreeze Vs Coolsculpting

Lipofreeze is a device for reducing fat deposits by cryolipolysis (fat splitting by cooling).

Both the method and the machine itself were created by scientists from Harvard (USA) D. Meinstein and R. Anderson.

The idea originated in the early 2000s: the basis was the discovery and study of the mechanisms of apoptosis (genetically programmed cell death), for which in 2002, scientists were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology.

Already in 2009, they created Lipofreeze device, which was later approved by the Office of the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) of America.

The goal: to reduce fat deposits by using low temperatures (even resistant to other methods – exercise, diets).

Contraindications: allergy to cold, pregnancy, breast-feeding, diabetes, obesity, scar in the treatment area.

How does Cryolipolysis® work?

Cryolipolysis is a non-invasive cooling of fat cells to stimulate lipolysis without damaging other tissues and structures.

High-precision cooling ensures safety, since it only affects fat cells, without affecting the skin, nerves and muscle tissue.

The proposed mechanism of action is the crystallization of lipids in fat cells:

  • Apoptotic death of fat cells, followed by cell decay and gradual release of lipids;
  • A natural inflammatory process leads to a thinning of the fat layer.

Contraindications to the procedure

Cryoglobulinemia is a very rare disease in which a large number of cryoglobulin proteins are present in the blood.
Proteins thicken on contact with the cold.
Symptoms may vary depending on the type and the affected organs, but include skin problems, fatigue, muscle / joint pain, breathing disorders, necrosis.

There Is No Required Down Time After A LipoFreeze Treatment

Paroxysmal cold hemoglobinuria.This is an extremely rare disease. Antibodies bind to erythrocytes at low temperatures.
Antibodies destroy red blood cells, moving along the body, and return to body temperature.

After cell destruction, hemoglobin is released into the blood and removed in the urine.
The acute degree of the disease is usually transient and goes untreated.

Cold agglutinin disease is a rare blood disease.

Cold agglutinins (antibodies) are produced in the bone marrow. High levels of these antibodies can occur when the functioning of the immune system is compromised.

They are activated by low temperatures and cause gluing of the red blood cells with which they are attached. This leads to an autoimmune reaction, which prematurely destroys the red blood cells (to hemolysis).
